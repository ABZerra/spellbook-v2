# Research Synthesis Framework

## Purpose

Use this framework to turn fragmented discovery evidence into one coherent input that discovery and definition artifacts can reuse without losing nuance, contradictions, or uncertainty.

## Source Normalization

- Capture each source with type, date or period, audience/segment, and strength caveats.
- Preserve the original wording of important evidence instead of flattening everything into one paraphrase.
- Normalize mixed inputs into comparable notes: interview findings, open-text survey themes, support patterns, sales observations, usability findings, and analytics summaries.

## Evidence Strength Vocabulary

Use qualitative strength labels instead of false precision:

- `Strong`: repeated across multiple sources or segments with clear supporting evidence.
- `Moderate`: supported by more than one source but still thin in sample size, recency, or consistency.
- `Directional`: useful signal, but from a narrow or incomplete evidence base.
- `Unknown`: cannot be rated honestly from the available data.

Always explain why a theme earned its label.

## Convergence Analysis

- Identify where multiple sources point to the same underlying problem, behavior, or desired outcome.
- Separate repeated symptoms from repeated causes.
- Prefer themes that explain user progress, blockers, and tradeoffs rather than just requested features.

## Contradiction Analysis

- Call out conflicting findings directly instead of smoothing them away.
- Distinguish true disagreement from measurement differences, sample differences, or time-window differences.
- Treat contradictions as inputs to discovery questions, not as reasons to invent certainty.

## Gap Detection

- List what the evidence does not answer yet.
- Mark missing segmentation, missing baseline metrics, missing recency, or missing behavioral evidence explicitly.
- Convert the most important gaps into discovery questions that can drive the next artifact.

## Segment-Difference Handling

- Note when a theme appears specific to a user type, journey stage, market tier, or product context.
- Keep segment-specific findings attached to the segment rather than promoting them to universal truth.
- If a segment split is only suspected, say so.

## Outlier Handling

- Preserve unusual findings that may matter strategically even when they are not broadly repeated.
- Label them as outliers or hypotheses unless evidence is stronger than it first appears.
- Use outliers to suggest follow-up research, not default scope.

## JTBD Candidate Derivation

- Convert strong or moderate evidence into candidate JTBD statements using `references/product/jtbd-framework.md`.
- Keep candidate JTBDs grounded in user progress, not solution requests.
- When evidence is weak or contradictory, keep the JTBD as `TBD` or mark it as a candidate rather than validated.

## No Default Numeric Scoring

- Do not default to numerical importance, satisfaction, or opportunity scoring.
- Prefer qualitative evidence strength and confidence notes unless the user already has a credible scoring model with supporting data.
- If numeric inputs are absent, do not fabricate them for polish.
